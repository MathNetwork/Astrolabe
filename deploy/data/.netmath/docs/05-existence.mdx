# Chapter 4. Existence of Almost Minimizing Varifolds

The principal theorem of this chapter is 4.11, establishing the existence of nonzero $k$ dimensional almost minimizing varifolds on arbitrary smooth compact Riemannian manifolds. We derive this existence theorem from a variational calculus based on generalized homotopy relations (4.1). These homotopy relations, motivated by the use of Vietoris homology in <objref id="90375244b674">[MM]</objref>, are well suited for the "slice and replace with a comparison surface" arguments common in the theory of currents and varifolds, and are therefore easier to use here than conventional continuous homotopies.

The variational calculus was developed by Almgren in <objref id="13151cb2445f">[AF2]</objref>, where he used it to prove that Riemannian manifolds support nonzero $k$ dimensional stationary integral varifolds. It is easy, however, to construct elementary examples which show that this is inadequate to establish regularity. Almost minimizing varifolds arise naturally in the existence theory as we have developed it. Through them we can simultaneously exploit the geometry of varifolds and the combinatorial operations of the variational calculus.

Our approach differs in several respects from that of Almgren. In particular, the equivalence of definitions established in 3.9 eliminates the need for the "VZ spaces" of <objref id="13151cb2445f">[AF2]</objref>, and by combining 4.11 and 3.13, we obtain Almgren's theorem on integral varifolds as a simple corollary. On the other hand, several theorems are based on ideas in <objref id="13151cb2445f">[AF2]</objref>; noteworthy are the use of the partition of unity in 4.3, the technical construction preceding 4.6, and aspects of the combinatorial argument in 4.10. We note that we may also apply our methods to prove a general analog (4.12) of the classical theorems of Morse-Tompkins <objref id="d4864032c0af">[MT]</objref> and Shiffman <objref id="56cbe53cdddc">[SM]</objref> on the existence of minimal surfaces of general critical type.

**Throughout this chapter we assume that $M$ is a compact submanifold of class 4 of $\mathbb{R}^n$.**

## 4.1–4.2 Homotopy Relations

### 4.1 Abstract homotopy relations

In this section we assume $(A,\rho)$ is a complete metric space, $B$ is a closed subset of $A$, and $\mathbf{m} : A \to \bar{\mathbb{R}}^+$ is a continuous function such that $A \cap \{x : \mathbf{m}(x) \leq r\}$ is bounded for each $0 \leq r < \infty$.

**(1) Cell complexes.**

**(a)** We define $I^m = \mathbb{R}^m \cap \{x : 0 \leq x_i \leq 1, \; i = 1, \ldots, m\}$ and $I_0^m = I^m \sim \operatorname{Int} I^m$.

**(b)** For each $j \in \mathbb{Z}^+$, let $I(1,j)$ denote the cell of $I^1$ whose 1-cells are the intervals $[0, 1 \cdot 3^{-j}]$, $[1 \cdot 3^{-j}, 2 \cdot 3^{-j}]$, $\ldots$, $[1 - 3^{-j}, 1]$, and whose 0-cells are the endpoints $[0]$, $[3^{-j}]$, $[2 \cdot 3^{-j}]$, $\ldots$, $[1]$.

**(c)** For each $m = 1, 2, \ldots$ and $j \in \mathbb{Z}^+$, $I(m,j) = I(1,j) \otimes \ldots \otimes I(1,j)$ ($m$ times) is a cell complex on $I^m$. $\alpha = \alpha_1 \otimes \ldots \otimes \alpha_m$ is a $p$-cell if and only if for each $i = 1, \ldots, m$, $\alpha_i$ is a cell in $I(1,j)$ and $\sum_{i=1}^m \operatorname{dimension}(\alpha_i) = p$. $I(m,j)_p$ denotes the set of all $p$-cells in $I(m,j)$.

**(d)** One has the usual boundary homomorphism $d : I(m,j) \to I(m,j)$ with $d(\alpha_1 \otimes \ldots \otimes \alpha_m) = \sum_{i=1}^m (-1)^{\sigma(i)} \alpha_1 \otimes \ldots \otimes d\alpha_i \otimes \ldots \otimes \alpha_m$ where $\sigma(i) = \sum_{p<i} \operatorname{dimension}(\alpha_p)$.

**(e)** We define the metric $\mathbf{d} : I(m,j)_0 \times I(m,j)_0 \to \mathbb{Z}^+$ by $\mathbf{d}(x,y) = 3^j \sum_{i=1}^m |x_i - y_i|$ for $x, y \in I(m,j)_0$.

**(f)** Whenever $\varphi : I(m,j)_0 \to A$, we define the **fineness of $\varphi$**, $\mathbf{f}(\varphi)$, by $\mathbf{f}(\varphi) = \sup\{\mathbf{d}(x,y)^{-1}\rho(\varphi(x),\varphi(y)) : x, y \in I(m,j)_0, \; x \neq y\}$. We say that $\varphi$ **has fineness $\delta$** whenever $\mathbf{f}(\varphi) \leq \delta$.

**(g)** Whenever $i, j \in \mathbb{Z}^+$, we define the natural projection $\mathbf{n}(i,j) : I(m,i)_0 \to I(m,j)_0$ as follows: for each $x \in I(m,i)_0$, $\mathbf{n}(i,j)(x)$ is that unique element of $I(m,j)_0$ such that $\mathbf{d}(x, \mathbf{n}(i,j)(x)) = \inf\{\mathbf{d}(x,y) : y \in I(m,j)_0\}$.

**(2) Homotopy maps.**

**(a)** Let $\delta > 0$. We say that $\varphi_1$ **is $m$ homotopic to $\varphi_2$ in $(A,B)$ with fineness $\delta$** if and only if there exist positive integers $k_1, k_2, k_3$ and a map $\psi : I(1,k_3)_0 \times I(m,k_3)_0 \to A$ such that $\mathbf{f}(\psi) < \delta$, and whenever $j = 1, 2$ and $x \in I(m,k_3)_0$, $\varphi_j : I(m,k_j)_0 \to A$, $\varphi_j[I_0(m,k_j)_0] \subset B$, $\psi([j-1],x) = \varphi_j(\mathbf{n}(k_3,k_j)(x))$, and $\psi[I_0(m+1,k_3)_0] \subset B$.

One notes that if $\varphi_1$ is $m$ homotopic to $\varphi_2$ in $(A,B)$ with fineness $\delta_1$ and $\varphi_2$ is $m$ homotopic to $\varphi_3$ in $(A,B)$ with fineness $\delta_2$, then $\varphi_1$ is $m$ homotopic to $\varphi_3$ in $(A,B)$ with fineness $\delta = \max\{\delta_1, \delta_2\}$.

**(b)** An **(m,$\mathbf{m}$) homotopy sequence of mappings into $(A,B)$** is a sequence of mappings $\varphi_1, \varphi_2, \ldots$ for which there exist positive numbers $\delta_1, \delta_2, \ldots$ such that $\varphi_i$ is $m$ homotopic to $\varphi_{i+1}$ in $(A,B)$ with fineness $\delta_i$ for each $0 < i \in \mathbb{Z}$, $\lim_i \delta_i = 0$, and $\sup \mathbf{m}(\varphi_i(x)) : x \in \operatorname{dmn}(\varphi_i), \; 0 < i \in \mathbb{Z}\} < \infty$.

**(c)** If $S_1 = \{\varphi_i^1\}$ and $S_2 = \{\varphi_i^2\}$ are $(m,\mathbf{m})$ homotopy sequences into $(A,B)$, then $S_1$ **is homotopic with** $S_2$ if and only if there exists a sequence $\delta_1, \delta_2, \ldots$ in $\mathbb{R}^+$ such that $\varphi_i^1$ is $m$ homotopic to $\varphi_i^2$ in $(A,B)$ with fineness $\delta_i$ for each $0 < i \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $\lim_i \delta_i = 0$. One checks that "is homotopic with" is an equivalence relation. An equivalence class of such sequences is an **(m,$\mathbf{m}$) homotopy class of mappings into $(A,B)$**.

**(3) Critical sequences of mappings.**

Let $\Pi$ be an $(m,\mathbf{m})$ homotopy class of mappings into $(A,B)$.

**(a)** We define $\mathbf{L} : \Pi \to \bar{\mathbb{R}}^+$ by setting $\mathbf{L}(S) = \limsup_{i \to \infty} (\sup \operatorname{image}(\mathbf{m} \circ \varphi_i))$ for each $S = \{\varphi_i\} \in \Pi$.

**(b)** Define the **critical level of $\Pi$**, denoted $\mathbf{L}(\Pi)$, by $\mathbf{L}(\Pi) = \inf\{\mathbf{L}(S) : S \in \Pi\}$.

**(c)** $S$ is a **critical sequence for $\Pi$** provided $S \in \Pi$ and $\mathbf{L}(S) = \mathbf{L}(\Pi)$.

**(4) PROPOSITION. Every $(m,\mathbf{m})$ homotopy class of mappings into $(A,B)$ contains a critical sequence.**

PROOF. For each $0 < j \in \mathbb{Z}$, we choose $S^j = \{\varphi_i^j\} \in \Pi$ such that $\mathbf{L}(S^j) \leq \mathbf{L}(\Pi) + j^{-1}$, and note that the fineness properties hold. Hence one may choose integers $l = n_0 < n_1 < n_2 < \ldots$ such that for each $0 < j \in \mathbb{Z}$, $\sup(\sup \operatorname{image}(\mathbf{m} \circ \varphi_i^j)) \leq \mathbf{L}(S^j) + j^{-1}$ for $i \geq n_j$, and $j^{-1} \geq \sup_{1 \leq k \leq j} \inf\{\delta : \varphi_i^k \text{ is } m \text{ homotopic to } \varphi_{i+1}^k \text{ in } (A,B) \text{ with fineness } \delta\}$ for $i \geq n_j$. One sets $\varphi_i = \varphi_i^1$ if $1 \leq i \leq n_2 - 1$, and $\varphi_i = \varphi_i^j$ if $n_j \leq i \leq n_{j+1} - 1$ for $j \geq 2$. Then $S = \{\varphi_i\} \in \Pi$ and $\mathbf{L}(S) = \mathbf{L}(\Pi)$.

**(5)** Let $a \in A$. We denote by $\pi_m^\#(A, \{a\})$ the $(m,\mathbf{m})$ homotopy classes of mappings into $(A, \{a\})$. In the usual way, one endows $\pi_1^\#(A, \{a\})$ with the structure of a group and $\pi_m^\#(A, \{a\})$ ($m \geq 2$) with the structure of an abelian group. We denote by $\pi_m(A, \{a\})$ the usual $m$ dimensional homotopy group of equivalence classes of continuous mappings $(I^m, I_0^m) \to (A, \{a\})$.

### 4.2 Definitions

Let $\Pi$ be an $(m,\mathbf{M})$ homotopy class of mappings into $(\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G), \{0\})$.

**(1)** We define $\mathbf{K} : \Pi \to \{K : K \subset \mathcal{V}_k(M) \text{ is compact}\}$. For $S = \{\varphi_i\} \in \Pi$, let $\mathbf{K}(S) = \{V : V = \lim_j |\varphi_{i_j}(x_j)| \text{ for some } i_1 < i_2 < \ldots \text{ and some } x_j \in \operatorname{dmn}(\varphi_{i_j})\}$.

**(2)** For each $S \in \Pi$, define the **critical set of $S$**, denoted $\mathbf{C}(S)$, by $\mathbf{C}(S) = \mathbf{K}(S) \cap \{V : \|V\|(M) = \mathbf{L}(S)\}$. One notes that $\mathbf{C}(S)$ is compact and nonempty.

(REMARK. The reader has noted that our homotopy maps have values in $\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G)$, a space of currents, while the critical set is a set of varifolds. One might prefer to simplify matters by eliminating the varifolds. But because the mass norm $\mathbf{M}$ is only lower-semicontinuous in the flat topology on currents, there seems to be no way to establish the analog of 4.3(2) below. On the other hand, one familiar with the complete development (4.3, 4.7, 4.10, 4.11) might hope to do better by considering only the group $\pi_m(\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G), \{0\})$, rather than $\pi_m^\#(\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G), \{0\})$. The problem with that approach is that it is not known how to prove an analog of (4) above. There are illustrative examples in Chapter 1.)

## 4.3 Stationary Varifolds and Critical Sequences

### 4.3 Theorem

**Let $k \geq 1$ and $\Pi$ be an $(m,\mathbf{M})$ homotopy class of mappings into $(\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G), \{0\})$.**

**(1)** If $\{\varphi_i\} \in \Pi$ and $f_t$ is a one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms on $M$, then $\{f_{\tau\#} \circ \varphi_i\} \in \Pi$ for each real number $\tau$.

**(2)** There exists a critical sequence $S \in \Pi$ such that $\mathbf{C}(S) \neq \emptyset$ and each $V \in \mathbf{C}(S)$ is stationary in $M$. Furthermore, if $S^* \in \Pi$ is any critical sequence, then $S$ may also be chosen so that $\mathbf{C}(S) \subset \mathbf{C}(S^*)$.

PROOF of (1). Our argument follows <objref id="13151cb2445f">[AF2, 12.5]</objref> very closely. The function $(\ast)$ $\mathbb{R} \times \widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G) \to \widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;G) \times \mathcal{V}_k(M)$, $(t,T) \mapsto (f_{t\#}T, |f_{t\#}T|)$ is continuous in the product topology. By 2.1(14b), the function $t \mapsto \|f_{t\#}(\varphi(x))\|(K)$ is upper-semicontinuous for each $x \in \operatorname{dmn}(\varphi)$ and each compact $K \subset M$. Using <objref id="af3f51d44df5">[FH1, 4.1.21]</objref>, one may find a finite partition $0 \leq t_0 < t_1 < \ldots < t_p = \tau$ such that (I) holds with $0, \tau$ replaced by $t_{j-1}, t_j$ for $j = 1, \ldots, p$. To finish the argument, we apply the function $(\ast)$ and the same estimation procedure as in the proof of 3.7.

PROOF of (2). Let us assume $k < \dim(M)$ and let $S^* = \{\varphi_i^*\}$ be a critical sequence for $\Pi$ satisfying the conclusions of 4.2(4), and $c = \sup \bigcup_i (\operatorname{image}(\mathbf{M} \circ \varphi_i^*)) < \infty$.

We define the compact sets $A = \mathcal{V}_k(M) \cap \{V : \|V\|(M) \leq c\}$, $A_0 = A \cap \{V : V \text{ is stationary in } M\}$, $A_1 = A \cap \{V : \mathbf{F}(V,A_0) \geq 2^{-1}\}$, and $A_i = A \cap \{V : 2^{-i} \leq \mathbf{F}(V,A_0) \leq 2^{-i+1}\}$ for $i = 2, 3, \ldots$

For each positive integer $j$, one chooses a positive integer $q_j$, varifolds $V(j,1), \ldots, V(j,q_j) \in A_j$, numbers $r(j,1), \ldots, r(j,q_j) \in \mathbb{R} \cap \{t : 0 < t < 2^{-j}\}$, vectorfields $g(j,1), \ldots, g(j,q_j) \in \mathfrak{X}_1(M) \cap \{g : |g|_1 \leq 1\}$, and open balls $U(j,i) = A \cap \{V : \mathbf{F}(V,V(j,i)) < r(j,i)\}$ for $i = 1, 2, \ldots, q_j$, such that $A_j \subset \bigcup_{i=1}^{q_j} U(j,i)$ and $\delta^{(1)}V(g(j,i)) \leq 2^{-1}\inf\{\delta^{(1)}V(g) : g \in \mathfrak{X}_1(M) \text{ and } |g|_1 \leq 1\} < 0$ for each $V \in U(j,i)$ and $i = 1, 2, \ldots, q_j$.

Using these choices, one constructs a continuous vectorfield $g : A \to \mathfrak{X}_1(M)$ and a continuous function $h : A \to I$ with the properties that $h(V) = 0$ if $V \in A_0$, $h(V) > 0$ if $V \in A \sim A_0$, and $\|f_{s\#}V\|(\mathbb{R}^n) < \|f_{t\#}V\|(\mathbb{R}^n)$ if $0 \leq t < s \leq h(V)$, where $f_t$ is the one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms generated by $g(V)$.

We define a function $H : I \times \widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G) \cap \{T : \mathbf{M}(T) \leq c\} \to \widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;G) \cap \{T : \mathbf{M}(T) \leq c\}$ by $H(t,T) = G(t,g(|T|),T)$ if $0 \leq t \leq h(|T|)$ and $H(t,T) = H(h(|T|),T)$ if $h(|T|) \leq t \leq 1$, where $G(t,h,T) = f_{t\#}(T)$. One verifies that $H(t,T) = T$ whenever $t \in I$ and $|T| \in A_0$, and $\mathbf{M}(H(1,T)) < \mathbf{M}(T)$ whenever $T \in \widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G)$, $\mathbf{M}(T) \leq c$, and $|T| \notin A_0$.

Recalling (1), we may define our desired critical sequence $S = \{\varphi_i\} \in \Pi$ such that $\varphi_i(x) = H(1, \varphi_i^*(x))$ for each positive integer $i$ and each $x \in \operatorname{dmn}(\varphi_i^*)$. This completes the proof.

## 4.4–4.7 Nontrivial Homotopy Classes

### 4.4 Estimates

Here we define several functions and obtain estimates for use in 4.5.

**(1)** For each $x \in I$ and $0 < j \in \mathbb{Z}$, define $g(x,j) \in \{0,1\}$ to be the $j$th term of the unique nonterminating binary expansion of $x$. Thus, $x = \sum_{j=1}^\infty g(x,j) 2^{-j}$ for $x \in I$ and $\limsup_{j \to \infty} g(x,j) = 1$ for $0 < x \leq 1$.

**(2)** For each $t \in I$, we define $A_t = I^m \cap \operatorname{Clos}\{x : \sum_{j=1}^\infty \sum_{i=1}^m 2^{-mj-i} g(x_{m-i+1},j) \leq 1-t\}$.

**(3)** For each $t \in I$ we define a function $f(m,m,t) : I^m \to I^m$ which is geometrically the diagonal retraction of $I^m$ onto $A_t \cup \{x \in I^m : x_i = 0 \text{ for some } i = 1, \ldots, m\}$.

**(4)–(5)** Technical definitions of face retractions $f(m,m-j,t)$ for $j = 1, \ldots, m-1$ and their compositions, with Lipschitz estimates.

### 4.5 Theorem

**Let $1 \leq k \leq m$ and $K = I^m$, and let us write $\mathbf{I}_{k,K}(\mathbb{R}^m;G) = \mathbf{I}_k(\mathbb{R}^m;G) \cap \{T : \operatorname{spt} T \subset K\}$. The mapping**

$$\boldsymbol{\delta}(t,T) = f(t,\cdot)_\#T, \quad 0 \leq t \leq m, \; T \in \mathbf{I}_{k,K}(\mathbb{R}^m;G)$$

**is continuous with respect to the $\mathbf{M}$ metric topology on $\mathbf{I}_{k,K}(\mathbb{R}^m;G)$.**

### 4.6 Theorem (<objref id="13151cb2445f">[AF2, 13.4]</objref>)

**The following groups are naturally isomorphic:**

$$H_{m+k}(M;G), \quad \pi_m(\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;G),\{0\}), \quad \pi_m^\#(\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;G),\{0\}),$$

$$\pi_m(\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G),\{0\}), \quad \pi_m^\#(\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G),\{0\}).$$

PROOF. The methods of <objref id="5f6db91922c2">[AF1]</objref> immediately imply that the homology group and the homotopy groups of $\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;G)$ are isomorphic. The methods of <objref id="5f6db91922c2">[AF1, 3.2]</objref> give a homomorphism of each homotopy group of $\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;G)$ into the homology group; one infers from the interpolation formula <objref id="5f6db91922c2">[AF1, 6.5]</objref> that these maps are surjective. To complete the proof, we replace the deformation $D$ <objref id="5f6db91922c2">[AF1, 5.2]</objref> by $\boldsymbol{\delta}$, so that by using a differentiable triangulation of $M$ rather than the simplicial complex of <objref id="5f6db91922c2">[AF1, 5]</objref>, we may apply the arguments of <objref id="5f6db91922c2">[AF1]</objref> to produce the isomorphism.

### 4.7 Corollary

**Whenever $H_m(M;G) \neq 0$, there exists $\Pi \in \pi_{m-k}^\#(\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G), \{0\})$ such that $\mathbf{L}(\Pi) > 0$. In particular, if $m = \dim(M)$, then $H_m(M;\mathbb{Z}_2) \neq 0$, so for each $k$, $1 \leq k \leq m$, there exists $\Pi \in \pi_{m-k}^\#(\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};\mathbb{Z}_2), \{0\})$ such that $\mathbf{L}(\Pi) > 0$.**

PROOF. Apply 4.6 and <objref id="5f6db91922c2">[AF1, 8.2]</objref>.

## 4.8–4.9 Covering Lemmas

The proof of the existence theorem 4.10 is in large part a complicated combinatorial argument. Part of the complexity arises from the need to untangle varifold behavior in very many annuli which may possibly overlap. This is accomplished by an argument from covering theory which we develop in 4.8 and 4.9.

### 4.8 Lemma

**Suppose $k$ and $m$ are positive integers, and $\mathcal{A} = \{\mathbf{A}(p_i, s_{ij}, r_{ij}) : i = 1, \ldots, k, \; j = 1, 2, \ldots, km\}$ is a collection of closed annuli in $\mathbb{R}^n$ such that $r_{ij} > s_{ij} > 2s_{i(j+1)} > 2r_{i(j+1)}$ for each $i = 1, \ldots, k$ and $j = 1, \ldots, km-1$. Then there exists a disjointed collection $C \subset \mathcal{A}$ such that for each $i = 1, \ldots, k$, $m = \operatorname{card} C \cap \{\mathbf{A}(p_i, s_{ij}, r_{ij}) : j = 1, 2, \ldots, km\}$.**

PROOF. Write $a(i,j)$ for $\mathbf{A}(p_i, s_{ij}, r_{ij})$. We choose $C$ as follows. Let $a(i_1,j_1)$ be some annulus in $\mathcal{A}$ of smallest diameter. We place $a(i_1,j_1)$ in $C_{i_1}$. The selection procedure is iterated: at each step we choose the smallest remaining annulus that doesn't intersect any previously selected annulus from the same index $i$. The selection of $C = \bigcup C_i$ is complete after $km$ choices.

### 4.9 Proposition

**For each $\sigma \in I(m,k)$, let $A(\sigma)$ be a disjointed collection of $(3^m)^{3^m}$ concentric closed annuli in $\mathbb{R}^n$ such that $a_1 \cap \boldsymbol{\mu}(r)[a_2] = \emptyset$ for every $a_1, a_2 \in A(\sigma)$ with $a_1 \neq a_2$ and every $1 \leq r \leq 2$. Then there exists a function $\alpha$ which assigns to each $\sigma \in I(m,k)$ an annulus $\alpha(\sigma) \in A(\sigma)$ such that $\alpha(\sigma) \cap \alpha(\tau) = \emptyset$ whenever $\sigma, \tau \in I(m,k)$, $\sigma \neq \tau$, and $\sigma$ and $\tau$ are faces of some cell $\gamma \in I(m,k)$. Furthermore, $\operatorname{card}(\operatorname{image} \alpha) \leq 3^{2m}$.**

## 4.10–4.13 Existence Theorems for Almost Minimizing Varifolds

### 4.10 Theorem

**Let $1 \leq k \leq \dim(M)$. If $\Pi$ is an $(m,\mathbf{M})$ homotopy class of mappings into $(\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G), \{0\})$, then there exists $V \in \mathcal{V}_k(M)$ such that the following three statements are true.**

**(1)** $\|V\|(\mathbb{R}^n) = \mathbf{L}(\Pi)$.

**(2)** $V$ is stationary in $M$.

**(3)** For each $p \in M$, there exists a positive number $r$ such that $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing in $M \cap \mathbf{A}_0(p,s,r)$ for all $0 < s < r$.

PROOF. **The theorem is trivial if $k = \dim(M)$ or $\mathbf{L}(\Pi) = 0$**; we take $k < \dim(M)$ and $\mathbf{L}(\Pi) > 0$. Let $S = \{\varphi_i\}$ be a critical sequence for $\Pi$ satisfying the conclusions of 4.3; i.e., $\mathbf{C}(S) \neq \emptyset$ and if $V \in \mathbf{C}(S)$, then $\|V\|(\mathbb{R}^n) = \mathbf{L}(\Pi)$ and $V$ is stationary in $M$. We shall assume that no $V \in \mathbf{C}(S)$ satisfies conclusion (3) of the theorem and obtain a contradiction by constructing $S^* \in \Pi$ with $\mathbf{L}(S^*) < \mathbf{L}(S)$.

We abbreviate $L = 3^m$, $c = L^L$.

**Part 1.** By the compactness of $M$ and our assumption, for each $V \in \mathbf{C}(S)$, there exists $p \in \operatorname{spt}\|V\|$ and positive numbers $r_1, \ldots, r_c, s_1, \ldots, s_c$ having the property that $r_i - 2s_i > 2(r_{i+1} + 2s_{i+1})$, $r_c - 2s_c > 0$, and $V$ is not $G$ almost minimizing in $M \cap \mathbf{A}_0(p, r_i - s_i, r_i + s_i)$ for $i = 1, 2, \ldots, c$.

**Part 2.** For each $V \in \mathbf{C}(S)$, there exists $\varepsilon(V) > 0$ with the property that for $j = 1, 2, \ldots, c$, each $\delta > 0$, and each $T \in \widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G)$ with $\mathbf{F}(V,|T|) < \varepsilon(V)$, there exists a sequence $T = T_1, T_2, \ldots, T_q$ in $\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M;\mathbf{M};G)$ with $\operatorname{spt}(T_i - T) \subset a_j(V)$, $\sup_i \mathbf{M}(T_i - T_{i-1}) \leq \delta$, $\sup_i \mathbf{M}(T_i) \leq \mathbf{M}(T) + \delta$, and $\varepsilon(V) < \mathbf{M}(T) - \mathbf{M}(T_q)$.

**Part 3.** $\mathbf{C}(S)$ is compact, so there exists a finite sequence $V_1, V_2, \ldots, V_\nu$ in $\mathbf{C}(S)$ such that $\mathbf{C}(S) \subset \bigcup_{i=1}^\nu \mathcal{V}_k(M) \cap \{V : \mathbf{F}(V,V_i) < 4^{-1}\varepsilon(V_i)\}$.

**Part 4.** We define positive numbers $\varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2, s, n_1, n_2, \ldots$

**Parts 5–20.** Through an elaborate combinatorial construction using the covering lemma 4.9, we construct a new homotopy sequence $S^* = \{\varphi_i^*\} \in \Pi$ by modifying each $\varphi_i$ on cells $\sigma$ of appropriate refinements $I(m,N_3)$ of $I^m$. The key idea is: wherever $\varphi_i(x)$ has mass near $\mathbf{L}(S)$, we can decrease the mass by exploiting the non-almost-minimizing property in the annuli provided by the covering argument, while maintaining the homotopy relations. After the construction, one verifies that $S^* \in \Pi$ and $\mathbf{L}(S^*) \leq \mathbf{L}(S) - \varepsilon_2$, a contradiction.

### 4.11 Theorem

**If $M$ is a compact Riemannian manifold of class 4 and $H_m(M;G) \neq 0$, then for each $k \leq m$, there exists $0 \neq V \in \mathcal{V}_k(M)$ such that the following four statements are true.**

**(1)** $V$ is stationary in $M$.

**(2)** For each $p \in M$, there exists a positive number $r$ such that $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing in $M \cap \mathbf{A}_0(p,s,r)$ for all $0 < s < r$.

**(3)** $\operatorname{Card}(M \cap \{x : V \text{ is not } G \text{ almost minimizing at } x\}) < \infty$.

**(4)** $V \in \mathcal{JV}_k(M)$.

PROOF. By the Nash imbedding theorem <objref id="8b3a7acdd32d">[NJ]</objref>, we may assume without loss of generality that $M$ is isometrically imbedded in some euclidean space. Clearly (2) implies (3), and (4) follows from (1), (2), and 3.13. (1) and (2) follow from 4.6 and 4.10.

### 4.12 Theorem (Morse-Tompkins-Shiffman analog)

**If** (a) $1 \leq k \leq n-1$; (b) $0 < \varepsilon < \infty$; (c) $C \in \mathbf{I}_{k-1}(\mathbb{R}^n;G)$, $\partial C = 0$; (d) for each $i = 1, 2$, there is a current $T_i \in \mathbf{I}_k(\mathbb{R}^n;G)$ with $\partial T_i = C$ such that $\mathbf{M}(T_i) < \mathbf{M}(T_i + S)$ for all $S \in \mathbf{I}_k(\mathbb{R}^n;G)$ with $\partial S = 0$ and $0 < \mathbf{M}(S) < \varepsilon$; **then there exists a varifold $V \in \mathbf{IV}_k(\mathbb{R}^n)$ such that**

**(1)** $V$ is stationary in $\mathbb{R}^n \sim \operatorname{spt} C$;

**(2)** $\|V\|(\mathbb{R}^n) > \max\{\mathbf{M}(T_1), \mathbf{M}(T_2)\}$;

**(3)** $\operatorname{spt} C \subset \operatorname{spt}\|V\|$;

**(4)** for each point $p \in \mathbb{R}^n \sim \operatorname{spt} C$, there exists a number $r$, $0 < r < \operatorname{distance}(p, \operatorname{spt} C)$, such that $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing in $\mathbf{A}_0(p,s,r)$ for all $0 < s < r$.

PROOF. (Sketch) One constructs a $(1,\mathbf{M})$ homotopy class $\Pi$ of mappings into $(\mathbf{I}_k(\mathbb{R}^n) \cap \{S : \partial S = C\}, \{T_1, T_2\})$ such that if $\{\varphi_i\} \in \Pi$, then $\varphi_i([0]) = T_1$ and $\varphi_i([1]) = T_2$ for each positive integer $i$. One observes that $\mathbf{L}(\Pi) > \max\{\mathbf{M}(T_1), \mathbf{M}(T_2)\}$. The proof now proceeds as in 4.10.

### 4.13 Remark

We may redo the covering argument (4.9, 4.10), using closed balls instead of annuli; one obtains a theorem similar to 4.11. We did not make this our principal theorem because it would then be more difficult to prove regularity at the points where the varifold is not almost minimizing (cf. 7.12).

**THEOREM. If $M$ is a $q$ dimensional submanifold of class 4 of $\mathbb{R}^n$ and $H_m(M;G) \neq 0$, then there is a finite positive integer $N = N(n,q)$, with the property that for each $k \leq m$, there exists a varifold $0 \neq V \in \mathcal{V}_k(M)$ such that the following three statements are true.**

**(1)** $V$ is stationary in $M$.

**(2)** $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing at each point of $M$ except possibly for a set of points whose cardinality does not exceed $N$.

**(3)** $V \in \mathcal{JV}_k(M)$.

In these dimensions, <objref id="3965488135e9"></objref> completely answers the general question: for what positive integers $k$ and $n$ does a smooth, compact, $n$-dimensional Riemannian manifold support a regular closed minimal submanifold of dimension $k$?

Classically the only case with satisfactory answers of great generality was $k = 1$ and $n$ arbitrary (existence of closed geodesics). The first breakthrough to higher dimensions without severe restrictions on the ambient manifold came in 1974, when a precursor of <objref id="3965488135e9"></objref> valid for $k = 2$ and $n = 3$ was established (announced in <objref id="cae77385ca90">[PJ2]</objref>, revised and distributed in [PJ3, PJ4, PJ5]). Much of the method used then was peculiar to $k = 2$. The present work develops new, more powerful and more general estimates, extending the regularity theory to the dimensions in <objref id="3965488135e9"></objref>. This is the first general existence theorem of this type for regular $k$-dimensional minimal surfaces when $k > 2$.


## Two Parts of the Development

Generally speaking, there are two large parts to the logical development.

**Part I — Existence (Chapters 3 and 4).** A very general existence theory for minimal surfaces, applicable on arbitrary compact Riemannian manifolds in all dimensions and codimensions.

**Part II — Regularity (Chapters 5, 6, and 7).** Special estimates necessary to establish <objref id="3965488135e9"></objref>.

The general existence theory has its roots in the work of Almgren <objref id="5f6db91922c2">[AF1, AF2]</objref>. Almgren demonstrated that on a compact manifold $M$, the homotopy groups of the integral cycle groups on $M$ are isomorphic to the homology groups of $M$. This led him to construct a variational calculus in the large analogous to that of Marston Morse, from which he concluded <objref id="13151cb2445f">[AF2]</objref> that $M$ supports a nonzero stationary integral varifold in all dimensions not exceeding $\dim(M)$.

Here we construct a similar variational calculus and prove a critical extension: namely, that $M$ supports nonzero stationary integral varifolds with an additional variational property, which we have called **almost minimizing** (<objref id="3a15437a88fe"></objref>). Intuitively, an almost minimizing varifold is one which may be approximated arbitrarily closely by integral currents which are themselves very nearly locally area minimizing. Almost minimizing varifolds are the principal objects of our investigation.

## The Role of Almost Minimizing

The study of almost minimizing varifolds began because Almgren's theorem on the existence of stationary integral varifolds is inadequate to settle the question of existence of **regular** minimal surfaces on manifolds. Varifolds which are only stationary and integral have in general essential singularities, possibly of positive measure. If, in addition, the varifold is almost minimizing, then it possesses strong local stability properties which yield estimates on the singular sets. In particular, these estimates imply the singular set is empty for hypersurfaces of $n$-dimensional manifolds, $3 \leq n \leq 6$, which is <objref id="3965488135e9"></objref>.

Thus, the regularity theory depends on careful analysis of **stable** surfaces (minimal surfaces whose second variation of area is nonnegative). For our purposes the salient property of stable surfaces is that their geometric configurations are considerably more restricted than those of general minimal surfaces.

## Historical Remarks

**$k = 1$ (Closed geodesics).** The classic case, settled in 1951 by Lyusternik and Fet <objref id="a32d5f2ef7a1">[LF]</objref>, who showed that an arbitrary compact manifold always supports a closed geodesic, possibly with self-intersections. In 1929 Lusternik and Schnirelmann <objref id="c734d194c893">[LS]</objref> proved that if $M$ is two-dimensional and simply connected, then there exist at least three closed geodesics without self-intersections.

**$k = 2$ (Minimal surfaces).** The first general theorem was the predecessor of <objref id="3965488135e9"></objref> in <objref id="cae77385ca90">[PJ2]</objref>. Since then, Sacks and Uhlenbeck <objref id="121c71999ef0">[SU]</objref> have studied minimal immersions of spheres: if $n \geq 3$ and the universal covering space of $M$ is not contractible, then there exists a smooth mapping of $S^2$ into $M$ which is a conformal minimal immersion, possibly with self-intersections. When $n = 3$, there will be no branch points. In a second development, Schoen and Yau <objref id="20112cb01826">[SY]</objref> proved that if $S$ is a Riemann surface, $f : S \to M$ is continuous, and the induced map $f_\# : \pi_1(S) \to \pi_1(M)$ is injective, then there is a branched minimal immersion $g : S \to M$ such that $g_\# = f_\#$ and $g$ minimizes induced area among all maps with the same action on $\pi_1(S)$.

**$k = n - 1$ (Hypersurfaces, area minimizing).** If $k = n - 1 \leq 6$ and the $k$-dimensional homology group of $M$ with coefficients in the integers does not vanish, then $M$ supports a closed minimal hypersurface. This follows from the methods of <objref id="af3f51d44df5">[FH1, chapter 5]</objref> applied to a homologically area minimizing $k$-dimensional homology class on $M$ (cf. 7.2). Also, in [LHB1], Lawson explicitly constructs examples showing that $M = S^3$ supports closed two-dimensional minimal submanifolds of arbitrarily high genus.


## Self-Containedness

The presentation is self-contained insofar as possible. Techniques from differential geometry and topology are included as needed (§4.6 excepted). Regarding geometric measure theory, the work is not fully self-contained; Federer's exhaustive treatment <objref id="af3f51d44df5">[FH1]</objref> makes a lengthy duplication redundant. The important topic of varifolds, which has appeared since <objref id="af3f51d44df5">[FH1]</objref>, is treated following the comprehensive monograph <objref id="387d01a389db">[AW1]</objref>. The theorems about varifolds needed for the arguments are listed in §2.4.

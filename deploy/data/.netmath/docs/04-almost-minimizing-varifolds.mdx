# Chapter 3. Almost Minimizing Varifolds

The primary purpose of this monograph is to find surfaces which are regular solutions to certain naturally-posed variational problems. For emphasis one might say that there are two independent problems: first, to find solution surfaces (of any reasonable kind); and second, to find regular solution surfaces. In practice, however, these two problems are intertwined, and one prefers a unifying concept, provided of course that the concept is both natural and manageable. As we have undertaken this study, such a unifying concept has been a special kind of surface. We have called this surface an **almost minimizing varifold**, and it has become a principal object of our investigation.

Roughly speaking an almost minimizing varifold is one which may be approximated arbitrarily closely by currents with a certain weak minimizing property: there is no finite sequence (of currents) with small mesh which begins with the approximating current and which much decreases mass. While this definition is novel, in the context of our type of variational problem, it is both natural and effective. We have given illustrative examples in Chapter 1; let us summarize here why almost minimizing varifolds seem both necessary and natural.

**(1)** We wish to find closed minimal surfaces on arbitrary Riemannian manifolds. Apparently a minimum-maximum argument in the right variational calculus is promising, but among what general class of surfaces does one apply this argument? Examples show that manifolds are too restrictive; they are not closed under the natural convergence and deformation operations of geometric measure theory, and the possibility of singularities may not be excluded even in very simple cases. Integral currents are more satisfactory than manifolds because they accommodate singularities conveniently, and a suitable compactness theorem is available (<objref id="af3f51d44df5">[FH1, 4.2.17]</objref>), but there is a serious drawback. Mass is only lower-semicontinuous in the flat topology; the critical surface which it seems the variational calculus should have produced may have vanished, all or in part. Thus one is led to varifolds, which accommodate singularities as well as currents, and for which mass is a continuous function in the weak topology.

**(2)** Now one seeks to prove regularity. On general varifolds, the only natural variational operation is a smooth deformation of space. But smooth deformations leave singularities unaffected, and there seems to be no way to preclude the existence of singularities by first variation arguments alone. There is no natural "cut and paste" operation on varifolds, which lack the boundary operator $\partial$ which currents possess. Thus to prove regularity we need more information. This information is provided by almost minimizing varifolds, which are varifolds closely approximated by integral currents which are almost locally area minimizing. By performing the "cut and paste" arguments on the approximating currents, one is sometimes able to infer information about the varifolds sufficient for regularity.

**(3)** Almost minimizing varifolds occur naturally. The variational calculus which we construct in Chapter 4 yields precisely varifolds which have the almost minimizing property.

**(4)** Almost minimizing varifolds have a rich structure. This chapter is devoted to developing the calculus of almost minimizing varifolds. The salient properties are these: almost minimizing varifolds are (locally) stable (3.3); they are integral varifolds (3.13); and there is a powerful construction (3.10, 3.11) which yields comparison surfaces which are also almost minimizing varifolds, but of an especially useful type. (These useful comparison surfaces are the limit of currents which are literally locally area minimizing.) We also establish (3.4–3.9) several equivalent definitions of almost minimizing which are convenient in applications; and in 3.12 we explore varifold tangent properties of almost minimizing varifolds.

**Throughout this chapter, $M$ is a properly imbedded submanifold of class 4 of $\mathbb{R}^n$, $U$ is a bounded, relatively open subset of $M$, $C$ is a compact set with $U \subset C \subset M$, and $G$ represents either $\mathbb{Z}$ or $\mathbb{Z}_\nu$. We denote by $\boldsymbol{\nu}$ any one of $\mathfrak{F}_C$, $\mathbf{F}$, or $\mathbf{M}$; i.e., $\boldsymbol{\nu}(S,T) = \mathfrak{F}_C(S-T)$ for all $S, T \in \widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M, M \sim U; G)$, or $\boldsymbol{\nu}(S,T) = \mathbf{F}(S,T)$, or $\boldsymbol{\nu}(S,T) = \mathbf{M}(S-T)$.**

**(1)** $\mathfrak{a}_k(U;\varepsilon,\delta;\boldsymbol{\nu};G) \subset \mathfrak{a}_k(U;\varepsilon',\delta';\boldsymbol{\nu};G)$ whenever $0 < \varepsilon \leq \varepsilon' < \infty$ and $0 < \delta' \leq \delta < \infty$.

## 3.4–3.9 Equivalent Formulations of Almost Minimizing

As we defined almost minimizing varifolds in 3.1(2), the approximating currents are in $\mathfrak{a}_k(U;\varepsilon,\delta;\mathfrak{F}_C;G)$, which means that mass-decreasing sequences must have small mesh in the $\mathfrak{F}_C$ norm. We could have replaced $\mathfrak{F}_C$ by $\mathbf{M}$ or $\mathbf{F}_U$, which would apparently produce a different notion of almost minimizing. In 3.9 we show that the notion of almost minimizing is essentially the same, no matter which norm is used. This is a convenient equivalence. The existence theory is somewhat simplified by considering only sequences which are fine in the $\mathbf{M}$ norm topology; we shall explore this further in Chapter 4. In our other applications, including those in this chapter and all of the regularity in Chapters 5–7, it is much easier to use the $\mathfrak{F}_C$ norm. The main reason is that smooth deformations of space induce transformations of currents which are continuous in the $\mathfrak{F}_C$ norm topology. A secondary reason is that there is sometimes less bookkeeping in our geometric constructions.

The proof of 3.9 is not difficult, but it is lengthy. First there are many currents to keep track of. Second we must estimate mass very carefully, and deformations which are routine in euclidean space become more delicate on manifolds. In 3.4 we develop the notation and describe the fundamental estimates on manifolds. In order to keep the details manageable, the argument is subdivided into lemmas 3.5–3.8.

### 3.4 Notation and estimates on manifolds

**(1) Metrics.** We define $\rho : M \times M \to \bar{\mathbb{R}}^+$. If $x, y \in M$, then $\rho(x,y) = \infty$ if $x$ and $y$ are in different components of $M$; otherwise, $\rho(x,y)$ is the infimum of the lengths of all Lipschitz curves in $M$ with endpoints at $x$ and $y$. It is well known that $\rho$ metrizes connected subsets of $M$.

**(2) Notation.** We define $\mathbf{u} : M \to (\bar{\mathbb{R}}^+)^M$, $\mathbf{u}(x)(y) = \rho(x,y)$ if $x, y \in M$. We let $\varphi : M \to \bar{\mathbb{R}}^+$, $\varphi(x) = \inf \mathbf{u}(x)[M \sim U] = \operatorname{dist}_M(x, M \sim U)$ for $x \in M$.

For each $t \in \mathbb{R}$, let

$$B(t) = M \cap \{x : \varphi(x) \geq t\}, \quad U(t) = M \cap \{x : \varphi(x) > t\}, \quad \partial B(t) = M \cap \{x : \varphi(x) = t\}.$$

For each $p \in M$ and $0 \leq s \leq r < \infty$, we define sets

$$B^*(p,s) = M \cap \{x : \rho(p,x) \leq s\}, \quad U^*(p,s) = M \cap \{x : \rho(p,x) < s\},$$
$$\partial B^*(p,s) = M \cap \{x : \rho(p,x) = s\}, \quad A^*(p,s,r) = M \cap \{x : s \leq \rho(p,x) \leq r\},$$
$$A_0^*(p,s,r) = M \cap \{x : s < \rho(p,x) < r\}, \quad D^*(p,s) = M \sim B^*(p,s).$$

**(3) Exponential map.** We summarize a few well-known facts about the exponential map. Let $p \in M$ and let $\exp_p : \operatorname{Tan}(M,p) \to M$ be the usual exponential map.

- **(a)** Near $0 \in \operatorname{Tan}(M,p)$, $\exp_p$ is a local diffeomorphism of class 2.
- **(b)** $\rho(x, \exp_p \circ \boldsymbol{\mu}(\lambda) \circ \exp_p^{-1}(x)) = (1-\lambda)\rho(x,p)$ whenever $-1 \leq \lambda \leq 1$, $x \in M$, and $\rho(x,p)$ is sufficiently small.
- **(c)** If $k \geq 1$ and $y \in \operatorname{Tan}(M,p)$, then the function $g_k(y)$, $g_k(y)(v,w) = \langle v, \Lambda_k D\exp_p^{-1}(y) \rangle \cdot \langle w, \Lambda_k D\exp_p^{-1}(y) \rangle$, defined for $v, w \in \Lambda_k \operatorname{Tan}(M,x)$, is symmetric, bilinear, and satisfies $g_k(0)(v,w) = v \cdot w$ and $Dg_k(0) = 0$. When $k = 1$, this is equivalent to the classical fact that in normal coordinates, near the origin, the Riemannian metric is euclidean up to first order.

Furthermore, for each positive number $\varepsilon$,

$$\|\Lambda_k D(\exp_p \circ \boldsymbol{\mu}(\lambda) \circ \exp_p^{-1})(x)\| \leq \lambda^k(1 + \varepsilon(1-\lambda))$$

whenever $0 \leq \lambda \leq 1$, $x \in M$, and $\rho(x,p)$ is sufficiently small.

**(4)** Let $q \in M$ and $0 < \varepsilon < 1$. Using (3), one verifies that there exists a neighborhood $Z$ of $q$ in $M$ such that if $p \in Z$, $W = \exp_p^{-1}[Z]$, and $E = \exp_p|W$, then the following six statements are true.

- **(a)** $E$ is a diffeomorphism of class 2 onto $Z$.
- **(b)** $Z$ is strictly geodesically convex [SM1, 9-81].
- **(c)** $(\operatorname{Lip} E)^k(\operatorname{Lip} E^{-1})^k \leq 2$.
- **(d)** $\operatorname{Lip}(\mathbf{u}(p)|Z) \leq 2$.
- **(e)** If $x \in Z$ and $0 \leq \lambda \leq 1$, then $E \circ \boldsymbol{\mu}(\lambda) \circ E^{-1}(x) \in Z$.
- **(f)** If $x \in Z$, $0 \leq \lambda \leq 1$, $k \geq 1$, and $v \in \Lambda_k \operatorname{Tan}(M,x)$, then $|\langle v, \Lambda_k D(E \circ \boldsymbol{\mu}(\lambda) \circ E^{-1})(x) \rangle| \leq \lambda^k(1 + \varepsilon(1-\lambda))$.

**(5)–(8)** Technical estimates for currents under homothety, boundary operations, and the retraction map $h(r)$ on manifolds. These provide the foundational mass estimates used in the lemmas below.

### 3.5 Lemma

**If** (a) $k \geq 1$, $0 < \delta < \infty$, $0 < r < \infty$, $0 < \varepsilon < 1$; (b) $Z \subset U$ is a relatively open set having the properties of 3.4(4), $p \in Z$, $B^*(p,r) \subset Z$; (c) $T \in \widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(B^*(p,r), \partial B^*(p,r); G)$, $\varepsilon\mathbf{M}(T) < k\delta$, $2\mathbf{M}(\partial T)rk^{-1} + \delta \leq \mathbf{M}(T)$, and $\|T\|\partial B^*(p,s) = 0$ for all $0 \leq s \leq r$; **then for every positive number $\beta$, there exists a sequence** $T = R_0, R_1, \ldots, R_m$ **in $\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(B^*(p,r), \partial B^*(p,r); G)$ such that** $\partial R_i = \partial T$ for $i = 0, \ldots, m$; $\mathbf{M}(R_i) \leq \mathbf{M}(T) + \beta$ for $i = 0, \ldots, m$; $\sup_i \mathbf{M}(R_i - R_{i-1}) \leq \beta$; $R_m = (\exp_p)_\#(\mathfrak{d}_0 \bowtie (\exp_p^{-1})_\# \partial T)$; and $\mathbf{M}(R_m) \leq 2rk^{-1}\mathbf{M}(\partial T)$.

The proof proceeds in 9 parts via an intricate inductive construction using the exponential map and homothety estimates from 3.4.

### 3.6 Lemma

**If $k \geq 1$, $m = \dim(M)$, $Z$ is an open subset of $U$ having the properties of 3.4(4), and $V \in \mathcal{RV}_k(M,Z)$, then for $\mathcal{H}^m$ almost all $x$ in $Z$,**

$$\|V\|\partial B^*(x,s) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{\varepsilon \downarrow 0} \|V\|A^*(x, s-\varepsilon, s+\varepsilon) = 0$$

**for all $0 < s < \infty$ for which $B^*(x,s) \subset Z$.**

The proof uses the Gauss lemma and Fubini's theorem to show the relevant tangent cone condition holds for $\|V\|$ almost all $x$ in $Z$.

### 3.7 Lemma

**If $V \in \mathcal{V}_k(M)$, $T$, $T^*$, $T_1, T_2, \ldots$ are elements in $\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M, M \sim U; G)$, $\bigcup_i \operatorname{spt}(T^* - T_i) \subset U$, $\lim_i T_i = T$, $\lim_i |T_i| = V$, then for every $\delta > 0$, there exist $V^* \in \mathcal{V}_k(M)$ and a sequence $S_1, S_2, \ldots$ in $\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M, M \sim U; G)$ such that** $\bigcup_i \operatorname{spt}(T^* - S_i) \subset U$, $\lim_i S_i = T$, $\lim_i |S_i| = V^*$, $|T| \llcorner \mathbf{G}_k(U) = V^* \llcorner \mathbf{G}_k(U)$, $V^* \llcorner (M \sim U) \times \mathbf{G}(n,k) = V \llcorner (M \sim U) \times \mathbf{G}(n,k)$, **and the following property holds: for each $i \in \mathbb{Z}^+ \sim \{0\}$, there exists a finite sequence** $R_0, R_1, \ldots, R_m$ **in $\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M, M \sim U; G)$ such that $R_0 = T_i$, $R_m = S_i$, $\bigcup_{j=1}^m \operatorname{spt}(T^* - R_j) \subset U$, $\mathbf{M}(R_j) \leq \mathbf{M}(T_j) + \delta$, and $\sup_j \mathbf{M}(R_j - R_{j-1}) \leq \delta$.**

Furthermore, if $\bigcup_i \operatorname{spt}(T^* - T_i) \subset K$ for some compact set $K \subset U$, then $V^* = |T|$.

### 3.8 Lemma

**Suppose $0 < L < \infty$, $0 < \delta < \infty$, $K$ is a compact subset of $U$, and $T \in \widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M, M \sim U; G)$. There exists $0 < \varepsilon < \infty$ such that whenever**

$$S_1, S_2 \in \widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M, M \sim U; G), \quad \mathfrak{F}_C(S_1 - S_2) \leq \varepsilon,$$
$$\operatorname{spt}(S_1 - T) \cup \operatorname{spt}(S_2 - T) \subset K,$$
$$\mathbf{M}(S_1) \leq L, \quad \mathbf{M}(S_2) \leq L,$$

**there exists a finite sequence $T_0, T_1, \ldots, T_m$ in $\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M, M \sim U; G)$ with** $S_1 = T_0$, $S_2 = T_m$, $\bigcup_j \operatorname{spt}(T - T_j) \subset U$, and $\sup_j \mathbf{M}(T_j - T_{j-1}) \leq \delta$.

### 3.9 Theorem

**Let $V \in \mathcal{V}_k(M)$.**

**(1) Each of these statements implies the one following it.**

- **(a)** $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing in $U$.
- **(b)** For each $\varepsilon > 0$, there exist $\delta > 0$ and $T \in \mathfrak{a}_k(U;\varepsilon,\delta;\mathbf{F};G)$ with $\mathbf{F}_U(V, |T|) < \varepsilon$.
- **(c)** For each $\varepsilon > 0$, there exist $\delta > 0$ and $T \in \mathfrak{a}_k(U;\varepsilon,\delta;\mathbf{M};G)$ with $\mathbf{F}_U(V, |T|) < \varepsilon$.
- **(d)** $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing in any relatively open subset $W$ of $M$ with $\operatorname{Clos} W \subset U$.

**(2) Let $p \in M$. The following three statements are equivalent.**

- **(e)** $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing at $p$.
- **(f)** There exists a neighborhood $U$ of $p$ such that for each $\varepsilon > 0$, there exist $\delta > 0$ and $T \in \mathfrak{a}_k(U;\varepsilon,\delta;\mathbf{F};G)$ with $\mathbf{F}_U(V, |T|) < \varepsilon$.
- **(g)** There exists a neighborhood $U$ of $p$ such that for each $\varepsilon > 0$, there exist $\delta > 0$ and $T \in \mathfrak{a}_k(U;\varepsilon,\delta;\mathbf{M};G)$ with $\mathbf{F}_U(V, |T|) < \varepsilon$.

PROOF. Clearly (1) implies (2) and (a) implies (b) implies (c). (e) follows from the argument of 3.3. (2) follows from 2.1(19) and 2.1(20). We show that (c) implies (d). If (d) is false, then there exists a relatively open subset $W$ of $M$ with $\operatorname{Clos} W \subset U$ and $\zeta > 0$ such that for each $\delta > 0$ and each $T \in \widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M, M \sim U; G)$ with $\mathbf{F}_U(V, |T|) < \zeta$, there exists a sequence $T = T_0, T_1, \ldots, T_m$ in $\widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M, M \sim U; G)$ with the usual properties but $\mathbf{M}(T) - \mathbf{M}(T_m) > \zeta$. Using 3.8, we refine the mesh to obtain a contradiction with (c).

## 3.12 Varifold Tangents

### 3.12 Theorem

**(1) Suppose $V \in \mathbf{V}_k(\mathbb{R}^n)$, $p \in \operatorname{spt}\|V\|$, and $P \in \operatorname{VarTan}(V,p)$. If $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing at $p$, then $P$ is $G$ almost minimizing in any bounded open subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$. If $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing in $\mathbf{A}_0(p,s,r)$ for some $0 < r \in \mathbb{R}$ and all $r > s > 0$, then $P$ is $G$ almost minimizing in any bounded open subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ whose closure does not include the origin.**

PROOF. We take $p = 0$. Suppose $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing at $0$. It suffices (3.2(4)) to show that $P$ is $G$ almost minimizing in $\mathbf{U}(0,R)$ whenever $0 < R \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\|P\|\partial\mathbf{B}(0,R) = 0$. Let $R$ be so chosen and $\varepsilon > 0$. Recalling 3.9(2), 2.1(19), and 2.1(14b), we choose $1 < r < \infty$, $0 < \delta < \infty$, and $T \in \mathfrak{a}_k(\mathbf{U}(0,Rr^{-1}); \varepsilon r^{-k-1}, \delta; \mathbf{M}; G)$ such that $\mathbf{F}_{\mathbf{U}(0,R)}(\boldsymbol{\mu}(r)_\#|T|, P) < 2^{-1}\varepsilon$, and conclude that $\boldsymbol{\mu}(r)_\#T \in \mathfrak{a}_k(\mathbf{U}(0,R); \varepsilon, \delta r^k; \mathbf{M}; G)$ and $\mathbf{F}_{\mathbf{U}(0,R)}(|T|, P) < \varepsilon$. The proof is similar in the annular case.

**(2)** We would not know whether the theorem above is true for varifolds on manifolds. The following partial result, derived using the methods above and well-known properties of homotheties, is, however, frequently adequate for applications.

**Suppose $V \in \mathcal{V}_k(M)$, $p \in \operatorname{spt}\|V\|$, $m = \dim(M)$, and $P \in \operatorname{VarTan}(V,p)$. If $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing at $p$ (resp., $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing in $\mathbf{A}_0(p,s,r) \cap M$ for some $0 < r < \infty$ and all $0 < s < r$), then for every $0 < R < \infty$, $0 < S < R \in \mathbb{R}$, and $\varepsilon > 0$, there exist $1 < r < \infty$, $\delta > 0$, and $T \in \widetilde{\mathcal{Z}}_k(M, M \sim \mathbf{U}(p, Rr^{-1}); G)$ (resp., $\boldsymbol{\mu}(r) \circ \boldsymbol{\tau}(p)_\#T \in \mathfrak{a}_k(\mathbf{A}_0(0, S, R) \cap \boldsymbol{\mu}(r) \circ \boldsymbol{\tau}(p)[M]; \varepsilon, \delta; G)$) with these properties:**

- **(a)** There exists a function $f : \mathbf{B}(0,2R) \cap \operatorname{Tan}(M,p) \to \mathbb{R}^{n-m}$ such that $U = \mathbf{U}(0,R) \cap \{(x,f(x)) : x \in \mathbf{B}(0,2R)\} = \mathbf{U}(0,R) \cap \boldsymbol{\mu}(r) \circ \boldsymbol{\tau}(p)[M]$ and the norm of $f$ in the class 3 topology is less than $\varepsilon$.
- **(b)** $|\mathbf{H}_{\boldsymbol{\mu}(r)} \circ \boldsymbol{\tau}(p)[M]; \cdot, \cdot)| \leq \varepsilon$ on $\mathbf{G}_k(U)$.
- **(c)** $\boldsymbol{\mu}(r) \circ \boldsymbol{\tau}(p)_\#T \in \mathfrak{a}_k(U; \varepsilon, \delta; G)$ (resp., $\boldsymbol{\mu}(r) \circ \boldsymbol{\tau}(p)_\#T \in \mathfrak{a}_k(\mathbf{A}_0(0,S,R); \varepsilon, \delta; G)$).
- **(d)** $\mathbf{F}_A(|\boldsymbol{\mu}(r) \circ \boldsymbol{\tau}(p)_\#T|, P) \leq \varepsilon$, where $A = \mathbf{U}(0,R)$ (resp., $A = \mathbf{A}(0,S,R)$).

## 3.13 Integrality Theorem for Almost Minimizing Varifolds

### 3.13 Theorem

**Let $V \in \mathcal{V}_k(M)$. If, for each $p \in U$, there exists a finite positive number $r$ with the property that $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing in $U \cap \mathbf{A}_0(p,s,r)$ for all $0 < s < r$, and if $V$ is stationary in $U$, then $V \in \mathcal{JV}_k(M,U)$. If $V$ is $G$ almost minimizing in $U$, then the condition that $V$ be stationary in $U$ is redundant.**

(REMARK. This is an important theorem with a surprisingly simple proof. The proof is simple because we are able to make good use of the class $\mathfrak{B}(V; \cdot, \cdot)$ of 3.10 and of the uniqueness properties of cones (2.4(6)). We have formulated the theorem in annuli because the natural conclusion of the existence theorem (4.11) takes this form.)

PROOF. The second sentence follows from 3.3. Let

$$E = U \cap \{x : V \text{ is not } G \text{ almost minimizing at } x\}.$$

Then $E$ has no accumulation point in $U$, and $\|V\|(E) = 0$ by 2.4(2) (otherwise $\Theta^k(\|V\|, x) = \infty$ for some $x \in E$). Let us set

$$\infty > \mu = \sup\{|\mathbf{H}(M; a, S)| : (a,S) \in \mathbf{G}_k(U)\}$$

and define $d = 4^{-1}\exp(-\mu \cdot \operatorname{diam}(U))$.

We show that $\Theta^k(\|V\|, p) \geq d$ for $\|V\|$ almost all $p \in U$, which together with 2.4(9) implies that $V \in \mathcal{RV}_k(M,U)$.

Assume $p \in U \cap \operatorname{spt}\|V\|$ and $\Theta^k(\|V\|, p) < d$. One chooses $0 < 2s < r < R < k\mu^{-1}$ and $V^* \in \mathfrak{B}(V; U, M \cap \mathbf{A}(p, 2^{-1}s, s); G)$ such that $\mathbf{B}(p,R) \cap M \subset U$, $\|V\|\mathbf{B}(p,r) \leq d\alpha(k)r^k$, $r^k \leq 2(r-s)^k$, and $\|V^*\|\mathbf{B}(p,r) = \|V\|\mathbf{B}(p,r)$. This is possible by 2.1(15), 3.11, and 2.5. Noting that $\delta^{(1)}V^*(g) \leq \mu\|V^*\|(|g|)$ whenever $g \in \mathfrak{X}(\mathbf{U}(p,R))$, we choose $q \in \mathbf{A}_0(p, 2^{-1}s, s)$ so that $\Theta^k(\|V^*\|, q) \geq 1$ and apply 2.4(3) to calculate

$$d\alpha(k)r^k \geq \|V\|\mathbf{B}(p,r) = \|V^*\|\mathbf{B}(p,r) \geq \|V^*\|\mathbf{B}(q,r-s) \geq \alpha(k)(r-s)^k \exp(-\mu(s-r)) \geq \alpha(k) 2^{-1} r^k \exp(-\mu \cdot \operatorname{diam} U) = 2\alpha(k) r^k d,$$

a contradiction.

We now show that $\Theta^k(\|V\|, p)$ is a positive integer for $\|V\|$ almost all $p$ in $U$. By 2.1(18d) it suffices to show that $\Theta^k(\|V\|, p)$ is a positive integer whenever $p \in U$, $S = \operatorname{Tan}^k(\|V\|, p) \in \mathbf{G}(n,k)$, and $\operatorname{VarTan}(V,p) = \{P\}$.

One chooses $Q \in \mathcal{V}_k(\operatorname{Tan}(M,p))$ and sequences $r_1, r_2, \ldots$ of positive numbers and $V_1, V_2, \ldots$ in $\mathcal{V}_k(M)$ such that

$$V_j \in \mathfrak{B}(V; U, M \cap \mathbf{A}_0(p, r_j, 2r_j); G),$$

$V_j$ is stationary in $U$, $|\delta^{(1)}V_j(g)| \leq \mu\|V_j\|(|g|)$ for all $g \in \mathfrak{X}(\mathbf{U}(p,r_1))$, $V_j \in \mathcal{JV}_k(M, M \cap \mathbf{A}_0(p, r_j, 2r_j))$, $r_j \downarrow 0$ as $j \to \infty$,

$$P = \lim_j \boldsymbol{\mu}(r_j^{-1}) \circ \boldsymbol{\tau}(p)_\# V,$$
$$Q = \lim_j \boldsymbol{\mu}(r_j^{-1}) \circ \boldsymbol{\tau}(p)_\# V_j,$$

$\|Q\|\mathbf{U}(0,3) = \|P\|\mathbf{U}(0,3)$, $\delta^{(1)}Q = 0$, $Q \llcorner \mathbf{G}_k(\mathbb{R}^n \sim \mathbf{A}(0,1,2)) = P \llcorner \mathbf{G}_k(\mathbb{R}^n \sim \mathbf{A}(0,1,2))$, $\|Q\|\mathbf{A}_0(0,1,2) \neq 0$, and $Q \in \mathcal{JV}_k(\mathbb{R}^n, \mathbf{A}_0(0,1,2))$.

All of this is possible by 3.11, 2.4(4), 2.4(7), and 2.5. It follows from 2.4(6) that $P = Q$, hence,

$$\Theta^k(\|Q\|, x) = \Theta^k(\|V\|, p)$$

for $\|Q\|$ almost all $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$, in particular for $\|Q\|$ almost all $x \in \mathbf{A}_0(0,1,2)$; hence, $\Theta^k(\|V\|, p)$ is a positive integer.

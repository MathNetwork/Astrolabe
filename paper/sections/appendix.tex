\section{knowledge.json Schema Specification}
\label{sec:app-schema}


The Astrolabe knowledge graph is stored as a single JSON file with two top-level dictionaries: \texttt{obj} (objects) and \texttt{mor} (morphisms). Each entity is keyed by its 12-character hexadecimal identifier.

\paragraph{Object fields.}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{llp{7cm}}
\toprule
Field & Type & Description \\
\midrule
\texttt{id}         & string (hex12)  & Unique identifier \\
\texttt{name}       & string          & Display name (ASCII) \\
\texttt{sort}       & string          & Object sort label (e.g., \texttt{theorem}, \texttt{definition}) \\
\texttt{status}     & string          & One of: \texttt{stated}, \texttt{proven}, \texttt{wip}, \texttt{review}, \texttt{open} \\
\texttt{statement}  & string          & Formal or informal statement (MDX with \LaTeX{} math) \\
\texttt{proof}      & string          & Proof content \\
\texttt{intuition}  & string          & Intuitive explanation \\
\texttt{notes}      & string          & Additional notes \\
\texttt{metadata}   & object          & Extensible dictionary for plugin data \\
\texttt{position}   & \{x, y, z\}    & Spatial coordinates (Canvas layout) \\
\texttt{created\_at} & ISO 8601       & Creation timestamp \\
\texttt{updated\_at} & ISO 8601       & Last modification timestamp \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Morphism fields.}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{llp{7cm}}
\toprule
Field & Type & Description \\
\midrule
\texttt{id}       & string (hex12) & Unique identifier \\
\texttt{source}   & string (hex12) & Source object identifier \\
\texttt{target}   & string (hex12) & Target object identifier \\
\texttt{sort}     & string         & Morphism sort label (optional; e.g., \texttt{uses}, \texttt{formalizes}) \\
\texttt{strict}   & boolean        & Whether the dependency is strict (default \texttt{true}) \\
\texttt{label}    & string         & Short edge label \\
\texttt{notes}    & string         & Relationship description \\
\texttt{metadata} & object         & Extensible dictionary for plugin data \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Forbidden fields.} The following fields are actively stripped during schema migration to prevent frontend-only or deprecated data from persisting: \texttt{style}, \texttt{confidence}, \texttt{tags}, \texttt{scope}, \texttt{source} (as a field name; distinct from the \texttt{source} morphism endpoint).

\paragraph{Display math format.} Display mathematics must use multi-line format with \texttt{\$\$} on its own line. Node names use pure ASCII text.

\section{Core Morphism Vocabulary: Complete Definitions}
\label{sec:app-mor-vocab}


Section~\ref{sec:dm-sort-system} introduces the four most important morphism sorts. This appendix provides the complete vocabulary of 12 core sorts. Each entry specifies the sort's semantics, categorical properties, constraints, rendering behavior, and an example. Sorts outside this vocabulary are valid---the system is open---but receive default rendering (solid arrow with autoColor) and carry no system-level semantic constraints.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{I. Structural Relations}

\begin{description}
\item[\texttt{inclusion}] $i: A \to B$. \emph{$A$ belongs to $B$; $B$ contains $A$.} Part-of relationship. Transitive: $A \xrightarrow{i} B \xrightarrow{i} C$ implies $A$ transitively belongs to $C$. Many-to-one and one-to-many permitted. Must form a DAG (no inclusion cycles). Rendered as nesting or clustering on Canvas. \emph{Example:} Definition~2.3 $\to$ Section~2.1 $\to$ Chapter~2.

\item[\texttt{formalizes}] $f: A \to B$. \emph{$A$ (formal declaration) is a machine-verified implementation of $B$ (natural-language knowledge unit).} A semantic specialization of inclusion. Many-to-one: multiple Lean declarations may jointly formalize one theorem. Source sort is typically \texttt{lean-*}; target sort is typically a natural-language sort (\texttt{theorem}, \texttt{definition}). Rendered as double-width colored line. \emph{Example:} \texttt{lean-theorem} ``Nat.Prime.exists\_infinite'' $\to$ \texttt{theorem} ``Euclid's theorem on infinitude of primes.''
\end{description}

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{II. Logical Relations}

\begin{description}
\item[\texttt{uses}] $u: A \to B$. \emph{$A$'s statement logically depends on $B$.} $B$ is a precondition for stating $A$. Transitive. Cycles permitted (mutual recursion exists in mathematics). Rendered as solid arrow. \emph{Example:} \texttt{theorem} ``Compactness Theorem'' $\to$ \texttt{definition} ``Open Cover.''

\item[\texttt{proof-uses}] $p: A \to B$. \emph{$A$'s proof uses $B$, but $A$'s statement does not depend on $B$.} Weaker than \texttt{uses}: $B$ is a proof technique, not a logical precondition. Does not affect statement-level dependency analysis. Rendered as dashed arrow. \emph{Example:} \texttt{theorem} ``Bolzano--Weierstrass'' $\xrightarrow{p}$ \texttt{lemma} ``Nested Intervals.''

\item[\texttt{implies}] $\iota: A \to B$. \emph{If $A$ holds, then $B$ holds.} Logical implication. Transitive. Antisymmetric: $A \Rightarrow B$ and $B \Rightarrow A$ together constitute \texttt{equivalent}. Source and target should be objects with truth values (\texttt{theorem}, \texttt{conjecture}, \texttt{proposition}). Rendered as thick solid arrow. \emph{Example:} \texttt{axiom} ``Axiom of Choice'' $\to$ \texttt{theorem} ``Zorn's Lemma.''

\item[\texttt{equivalent}] $e: A \rightleftharpoons B$. \emph{$A$ and $B$ are logically equivalent.} Symmetric: creating $A \to B$ with sort \texttt{equivalent} automatically creates the reverse $B \to A$. Transitive: equivalence classes form. Rendered as double-headed arrow or undirected line. \emph{Example:} Zorn's Lemma $\leftrightarrow$ Axiom of Choice $\leftrightarrow$ Well-Ordering Theorem.

\item[\texttt{contradicts}] $c: A \to B$. \emph{$A$ and $B$ cannot both hold (in the same axiomatic system).} Symmetric (auto-generates reverse). \emph{Not} transitive: $A$ contradicts $B$ and $B$ contradicts $C$ does not imply $A$ contradicts $C$. Rendered as red line. \emph{Example:} Continuum Hypothesis $\leftrightarrow$ $\neg$Continuum Hypothesis.
\end{description}

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{III. Semantic Relations}

\begin{description}
\item[\texttt{generalizes}] $g: A \to B$. \emph{$A$ is a generalization of $B$; $B$ is a special case of $A$.} Antisymmetric; transitive; forms a partial order. Direction: from general to specific. Rendered as downward gradient arrow. \emph{Example:} Banach Fixed Point Theorem $\to$ Brouwer Fixed Point Theorem.

\item[\texttt{specializes}] $s: A \to B$. \emph{$A$ is a special case of $B$.} The reverse of \texttt{generalizes}. Included for readability: users may find it more natural to say ``$A$ specializes $B$'' than ``$B$ generalizes $A$.''

\item[\texttt{annotates}] $a: A \to B$. \emph{$A$ is a comment, remark, or supplementary note on $B$.} Non-logical: removing $A$ does not affect $B$'s validity. Not transitive. Rendered as light gray dashed line. \emph{Example:} \texttt{remark} ``This proof can be shortened'' $\to$ \texttt{theorem} ``Main Theorem.''

\item[\texttt{motivates}] $m: A \to B$. \emph{$A$ is the motivation or background for $B$; $B$ was created in response to $A$.} Not transitive. Rendered with a distinctive icon (e.g., lightbulb). \emph{Example:} \texttt{conjecture} ``Poincar\'{e} Conjecture'' $\to$ \texttt{theorem} ``Perelman's Theorem.''
\end{description}

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{IV. Meta Relations}

\begin{description}
\item[\texttt{cites}] $c: A \to B$. \emph{$A$ cites $B$ at the bibliographic level.} Not transitive. Carries no logical or semantic weight; purely a provenance record. Rendered as thin gray line. \emph{Example:} \texttt{remark} ``Pitts 1981'' $\to$ \texttt{definition} ``Varifold Variation.''
\end{description}

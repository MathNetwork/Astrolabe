**Lemma 1.2 (Uniqueness of identity).** The identity morphism at each object is unique. If $e \in \text{Hom}(A, A)$ satisfies $f \circ e = f$ for all $f$ with domain $A$ and $e \circ g = g$ for all $g$ with codomain $A$, then $e = \text{id}_A$.

*Proof.* $e = e \circ \text{id}_A = \text{id}_A$, where the first equality uses the right-unit property of $\text{id}_A$ and the second uses the left-unit property of $e$. $\square$

**Example 1.3.** The category $\textbf{Set}$ has sets as objects, functions as morphisms, and function composition as composition. The category $\textbf{Grp}$ has groups as objects and group homomorphisms as morphisms.

**Definition 1.4 (Initial object).** An object $I \in \mathcal{C}$ is *initial* if for every object $A$ there exists a unique morphism $I \to A$.

**Proposition 1.5 (Uniqueness of initial objects).** If $I$ and $I'$ are both initial in $\mathcal{C}$, then they are uniquely isomorphic: there exists a unique isomorphism $I \xrightarrow{\sim} I'$.

*Proof.* By initiality of $I$ there is a unique $f : I \to I'$; by initiality of $I'$ there is a unique $g : I' \to I$. Then $g \circ f : I \to I$ must equal $\text{id}_I$ by uniqueness, and similarly $f \circ g = \text{id}_{I'}$. $\square$

## 1.2 Functors

**Definition 1.6 (Functor).** Let $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{D}$ be categories. A *functor* $F : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ consists of:
1. A map $F : \text{Ob}(\mathcal{C}) \to \text{Ob}(\mathcal{D})$ on objects.
2. For each pair $A, B \in \text{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$, a map $F_{A,B} : \text{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A, B) \to \text{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(A), F(B))$ on morphisms.

subject to:

- **Identity preservation.** $F(\text{id}_A) = \text{id}_{F(A)}$ for all $A$.
- **Composition preservation.** $F(g \circ f) = F(g) \circ F(f)$ for all composable $f, g$.

**Definition 1.7 (Faithful functor).** A functor $F : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ is *faithful* if each map $F_{A,B} : \text{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A, B) \to \text{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(A), F(B))$ is injective.

**Definition 1.8 (Full functor).** A functor $F$ is *full* if each $F_{A,B}$ is surjective. A functor that is both full and faithful is *fully faithful*.

**Proposition 1.9 (Composition of functors).** If $F : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ and $G : \mathcal{D} \to \mathcal{E}$ are functors, then $G \circ F : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{E}$ defined by $(G \circ F)(A) = G(F(A))$ and $(G \circ F)(f) = G(F(f))$ is a functor.

*Proof.* Identity: $(G \circ F)(\text{id}_A) = G(F(\text{id}_A)) = G(\text{id}_{F(A)}) = \text{id}_{G(F(A))}$. Composition: $(G \circ F)(g \circ f) = G(F(g \circ f)) = G(F(g) \circ F(f)) = G(F(g)) \circ G(F(f))$. $\square$

**Lemma 1.10.** The composition of faithful functors is faithful.

*Proof.* If $F$ and $G$ are faithful and $(G \circ F)(f_1) = (G \circ F)(f_2)$, then $G(F(f_1)) = G(F(f_2))$; faithfulness of $G$ gives $F(f_1) = F(f_2)$; faithfulness of $F$ gives $f_1 = f_2$. $\square$

**Proposition 1.11 (Identity functor).** For any category $\mathcal{C}$, the *identity functor* $\text{Id}_{\mathcal{C}} : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C}$ defined by $\text{Id}(A) = A$ and $\text{Id}(f) = f$ is a functor. It is the identity for functor composition: $F \circ \text{Id} = F$ and $\text{Id} \circ F = F$.

**Proposition 1.12 (Functor composition is associative).** $(H \circ G) \circ F = H \circ (G \circ F)$ for all composable functors $F, G, H$.

*Proof.* On objects and morphisms, both sides evaluate to $H(G(F(-)))$. $\square$

## 1.3 Natural Transformations

**Definition 1.13 (Natural transformation).** Let $F, G : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$ be functors. A *natural transformation* $\eta : F \Rightarrow G$ is a family of morphisms $\{\eta_A : F(A) \to G(A)\}_{A \in \text{Ob}(\mathcal{C})}$ such that for every morphism $f : A \to B$ in $\mathcal{C}$, the following *naturality square* commutes:

$$
G(f) \circ \eta_A = \eta_B \circ F(f)
$$

Each $\eta_A$ is called a *component* of $\eta$.

**Definition 1.14 (Natural isomorphism).** A natural transformation $\eta : F \Rightarrow G$ is a *natural isomorphism* if every component $\eta_A$ is an isomorphism in $\mathcal{D}$.

**Proposition 1.15 (Vertical composition).** Given $\eta : F \Rightarrow G$ and $\mu : G \Rightarrow H$ where $F, G, H : \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$, the *vertical composition* $\mu \cdot \eta : F \Rightarrow H$ defined by $(\mu \cdot \eta)_A := \mu_A \circ \eta_A$ is a natural transformation.

*Proof.* For $f : A \to B$:

$$
H(f) \circ (\mu_A \circ \eta_A) = (H(f) \circ \mu_A) \circ \eta_A = (\mu_B \circ G(f)) \circ \eta_A = \mu_B \circ (G(f) \circ \eta_A) = \mu_B \circ (\eta_B \circ F(f)) = (\mu_B \circ \eta_B) \circ F(f)
$$

using naturality of $\mu$ and $\eta$ respectively. $\square$

**Proposition 1.16 (Identity natural transformation).** For any functor $F$, the family $\text{id}^F_A := \text{id}_{F(A)}$ is a natural transformation $F \Rightarrow F$, and it is the identity for vertical composition.

**Theorem 1.17 (Functor category).** For categories $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{D}$, the *functor category* $[\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{D}]$ has:
- Objects: functors $\mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{D}$.
- Morphisms: natural transformations.
- Composition: vertical composition (Proposition 1.15).
- Identity: identity natural transformation (Proposition 1.16).

This is a well-defined category: associativity and unit laws follow from those in $\mathcal{D}$.

## 1.4 Signatures and Free Categories

**Definition 1.18 (Signature / Quiver).** A *signature* (also called *quiver* or *directed graph*) is a quadruple $\Gamma = (V, E, s, t)$ where:
- $V$ is a set of *vertices*.
- $E$ is a set of *edges*.
- $s, t : E \to V$ assign to each edge its *source* and *target*.

A signature is the data that generates a category: vertices become objects, edges become generating morphisms.

**Definition 1.19 (Signature morphism).** A *signature morphism* $\phi : \Gamma \to \Gamma'$ is a pair $(\phi_V, \phi_E)$ where $\phi_V : V \to V'$, $\phi_E : E \to E'$, and:

$$
s'(\phi_E(e)) = \phi_V(s(e)), \quad t'(\phi_E(e)) = \phi_V(t(e))
$$

for all $e \in E$.

**Proposition 1.20 (Category of signatures).** Signatures and signature morphisms form a category $\textbf{Sig}$:
- Composition: $(\psi \circ \phi)_V = \psi_V \circ \phi_V$ and $(\psi \circ \phi)_E = \psi_E \circ \phi_E$.
- Identity: $(\text{id}_V, \text{id}_E)$.

*Proof.* Source/target preservation of the composite follows from that of each factor. Associativity and unit laws are inherited from $\textbf{Set}$. $\square$

**Definition 1.21 (Path).** Let $\Gamma = (V, E, s, t)$ be a signature. A *path* of length $n$ from $v$ to $w$ is a sequence of edges $(e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n)$ with $s(e_1) = v$, $t(e_n) = w$, and $t(e_i) = s(e_{i+1})$ for $1 \leq i < n$. The *empty path* $\epsilon_v$ at vertex $v$ has length $0$.

**Definition 1.22 (Free category).** The *free category* $\text{Free}(\Gamma)$ on a signature $\Gamma = (V, E, s, t)$ is the category with:
- Objects: $\text{Ob}(\text{Free}(\Gamma)) = V$.
- Morphisms: $\text{Hom}(v, w)$ = set of all paths from $v$ to $w$.
- Composition: path concatenation. Given path $p : v \to w$ and $q : w \to x$, their composite $q \circ p$ is the concatenation of $p$ followed by $q$.
- Identity: empty path $\epsilon_v$ at each vertex $v$.

**Proposition 1.23.** $\text{Free}(\Gamma)$ is a well-defined category.

*Proof.*
1. *Composition closure*: concatenating a path $v \to w$ with a path $w \to x$ yields a path $v \to x$.
2. *Associativity*: list concatenation is associative.
3. *Unit laws*: concatenating the empty list on either side is the identity operation. $\square$

**Theorem 1.24 (Universal property of free categories).** Let $\Gamma$ be a signature and $\mathcal{C}$ a category. For any signature morphism $\phi : \Gamma \to U(\mathcal{C})$ (where $U(\mathcal{C})$ is the underlying signature of $\mathcal{C}$), there exists a unique functor $\hat{\phi} : \text{Free}(\Gamma) \to \mathcal{C}$ such that $\hat{\phi}$ restricted to generators equals $\phi$.

*Proof.* Define $\hat{\phi}(v) = \phi_V(v)$ on objects. On a path $(e_1, \ldots, e_n)$, define $\hat{\phi}(e_1, \ldots, e_n) = \phi_E(e_n) \circ \cdots \circ \phi_E(e_1)$, and $\hat{\phi}(\epsilon_v) = \text{id}_{\phi_V(v)}$. Identity and composition preservation follow from the definitions. Uniqueness: any functor agreeing on generators must agree on all paths by induction on path length. $\square$

**Corollary 1.25.** $\text{Free} : \textbf{Sig} \to \textbf{Cat}$ is a functor. It is left adjoint to the forgetful functor $U : \textbf{Cat} \to \textbf{Sig}$.

**Proposition 1.26 (Free functor preserves composition).** For signature morphisms $\phi : \Gamma_1 \to \Gamma_2$ and $\psi : \Gamma_2 \to \Gamma_3$:

$$
\text{Free}(\psi \circ \phi) = \text{Free}(\psi) \circ \text{Free}(\phi)
$$

*Proof.* Both sides agree on generators by definition. By the universal property, they must be equal. $\square$
